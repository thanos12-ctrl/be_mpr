[124] Abstract
[127] The rapid expansion of Massive Open Online Courses (MOOCs) has made education widely accessible, yet their prevalent "one-size-fits-all" structure fails to address individual learner needs. This research gap highlights the demand for more dynamic and responsive educational systems. This project proposes an intelligent tutoring platform that leverages artificial intelligence to bridge this personalization gap. We employ a Reinforcement Learning (RL) model to create truly adaptive learning journeys for students. The RL agent analyzes real-time user interactions, such as answer correctness and time spent, to construct a comprehensive state representation of the student's knowledge. Based on this state, the agent's policy determines the optimal subsequent action, whether to introduce new material, recommend revision, or assign a quiz. The system's reward function is designed to incentivize actions that lead to efficient mastery and long-term knowledge retention. By learning an effective teaching strategy from data, the platform moves beyond the static curricula of existing systems. The ultimate goal is to develop a scalable solution that acts as a personal AI tutor for every learner, making education more engaging, effective, and tailored to individual learning paces and styles. This work demonstrates a practical application of RL to adapt educational content, addressing a key limitation in the worldwide digital classroom.

[236]  Introduction
[237] 1.1 Introduction
[238] The field of digital education is undergoing a significant transformation, with the global online education market experiencing unprecedented growth. Projections indicate a market value of over $880 billion by 2032, expanding at a notable annual rate [2]. This digital revolution in learning presents immense opportunities to reshape how knowledge is delivered and acquired, not only by making education more accessible across geography and time, but also by enabling data-driven teaching strategies that were impossible in conventional classrooms. However, despite this rapid expansion, a critical deficiency persists within the majority of existing e-learning platforms. Most of these systems are built on traditional, outdated pedagogical models that offer a static, one-size-fits-all curriculum and rely heavily on fixed sequences of lessons, generic quizzes, and uniform pacing. This approach fundamentally fails to acknowledge the diverse and individual nature of learning. Each student possesses a unique pace, distinct learning style, and varying levels of prior knowledge, which are largely ignored by rigid, linear educational paths. As a result, advanced learners are often under-challenged while struggling learners are pushed forward without sufficient reinforcement, causing disengagement, frustration, and weak long-term retention. 
[239] This creates a significant “personalization gap,” where the full potential of digital education to cater to individual learners remains largely untapped, leading to inefficiencies and suboptimal learning outcomes. To close this gap, modern systems must move beyond passive content delivery toward adaptive learning architectures that continuously model student performance, estimate concept mastery, and dynamically adjust question difficulty and learning flow in real time. Integrating intelligent feedback loops, progress analytics, and instructor-facing intervention tools can transform digital platforms from static repositories into responsive learning ecosystems. 
[240] In this emerging paradigm, personalization is no longer a premium feature but a foundational requirement for improving engagement, learning efficiency, and measurable academic success at scale.
[241] 1.2 Aims & Objective
[242] The primary aim of this project is to bridge the critical personalization gap in the current e-learning landscape by replacing static instruction with intelligent adaptation. We propose to develop an AI-driven adaptive learning platform that creates genuinely personalized educational journeys for every student, rather than forcing all learners through the same fixed sequence of lessons and assessments.
[243] Our system is designed to move beyond the constraints of traditional, linear curricula and provide a dynamic learning environment that continuously responds to learner behavior in real time. By analyzing performance patterns, pace, response confidence, and concept-level mastery signals, the platform will tailor both what the student learns next and how that content is delivered. In doing so, the project aims to improve not only short-term quiz performance but also deeper understanding, sustained engagement, and long-term academic progress.
[244] The core objectives to achieve this aim are:
[245] To design and build an intelligent system that accurately understands and models each student’s individual learning pace, current knowledge state, and evolving mastery profile over time.
[246] To implement a Reinforcement Learning (RL) model capable of identifying specific knowledge gaps, conceptual misunderstandings, and difficulty mismatches as a student interacts with the platform.
[247] To develop a mechanism that dynamically guides each learner along the most efficient and effective path to subject mastery, using AI recommendations to sequence lessons, adjust challenge levels, and optimize progression decisions.
[248] To create a seamless and intuitive user-facing portal that delivers this personalized experience, thereby increasing student engagement, improving long-term knowledge retention, and reducing dropout rates.
[252] 1.3 Scope
[253] The scope of this project encompasses the end-to-end design, development, and initial evaluation of a web-based platform for personalized academic support that can adapt to individual learner behavior in real time. The system’s core functionality will be powered by a Reinforcement Learning engine trained on the large-scale EdNet dataset, which includes over 130 million real-world student interaction logs and provides a strong empirical foundation for modeling learning dynamics at scale. 
[254] Within the platform, students will engage with structured educational content and assessments, while the system continuously analyzes performance indicators such as answer correctness, response time, and interaction history to infer mastery levels and learning pace. Based on this evidence, the platform will generate a personalized learning trajectory for each user instead of following a fixed, one-directional curriculum. 
[255] The system will be designed to recommend whether a learner should progress to new concepts, revisit prior material for reinforcement, or attempt targeted quizzes to consolidate understanding and improve retention. The initial implementation will focus on subjects and concept structures aligned with the EdNet dataset to ensure reliable training and practical deployment feasibility. The intended user base is broad and inclusive, covering learners who need additional support with difficult concepts as well as high-performing students who want to move faster through content with appropriately challenging recommendations.

[266] 2.1 Domain Explanation
[267] This project is positioned at the intersection of three critical and rapidly converging domains: e-learning, adaptive learning systems, and artificial intelligence. E-learning, or electronic learning, refers to the delivery of instructional content, assessments, and academic support through digital platforms and online resources.
[268]  Although this model has significantly broadened access to education by reducing geographic and logistical barriers, its pedagogical impact is often constrained by static content sequencing and uniform instructional pathways. Adaptive learning systems represent the next major step in this evolution by introducing responsiveness into the educational process. Rather than presenting identical material to every learner, these systems continuously personalize the learning experience by adjusting content order, difficulty level, and reinforcement strategy according to each student’s observed performance. The intelligence that enables this dynamic adaptation is typically provided by AI-driven decision models. In this project, we specifically focus on Reinforcement Learning (RL), a machine learning paradigm centered on sequential decision-making under uncertainty.
[269]  In an RL framework, an “agent” (our AI model) interacts with an “environment” (the learning platform and student interaction flow), takes actions, and receives feedback in the form of rewards. Over repeated interactions, the agent learns a policy that maximizes cumulative long-term reward rather than short-term correctness alone. In educational terms, this means the system can learn to choose the most effective pedagogical action at each moment, such as introducing a new concept, assigning targeted revision, or selecting an appropriately challenging quiz item. This makes RL especially suitable for personalized education, where the goal is not only to answer what a student should learn, but also when and at what level of difficulty they should learn it for the best long-term outcomes.
[271] 2.2 Review of Existing Systems
[272] The current landscape of online education is primarily shaped by Learning Management Systems (LMS) such as Moodle, Canvas, and Blackboard, along with large-scale MOOC platforms like Coursera and edX [4]. 
[273] These ecosystems are highly effective in delivering, organizing, and scaling educational content, enabling millions of learners to access structured courses across disciplines and institutions. Their infrastructure supports curriculum management, assessment workflows, instructor coordination, and broad content distribution with strong operational reliability. In most cases, however, the instructional model remains standardized: students are guided through the same predefined sequence of lectures, readings, and assignments, regardless of individual differences in prior knowledge, learning speed, or conceptual difficulty patterns.
[274]  Although some platforms now include limited personalization features, such as elective pathways, recommended courses, or basic progress dashboards, these enhancements are generally layered on top of a fundamentally static pedagogical core. The underlying learning trajectory itself is rarely adaptive. In practice, this means the system does not continuously interpret real-time learner signals to re-order topics, calibrate challenge levels, or provide strategically timed reinforcement. As a result, content progression is mostly rule-based rather than intelligence-driven, and the platform cannot respond dynamically when a student needs remediation, acceleration, or concept-specific intervention. This fixed-path design is one of the central limitations of current online learning systems and highlights the need for AI-powered adaptive frameworks that personalize instructional decisions at the level of each learner interaction.
[275] 2.3 Limitations of Existing Systems/ Research Gaps
[276] The core weakness of most current e-learning platforms lies in their persistent “one-size-fits-all” design. By enforcing a uniform pace and sequence for all learners, these systems systematically misalign instruction with individual needs. Students who struggle with foundational concepts are pushed forward before achieving mastery, allowing early misconceptions to accumulate into larger knowledge deficits. At the same time, high-performing learners are constrained by repetitive, low-challenge material, which reduces motivation and undermines meaningful progression. The result is a predictable pattern of reduced engagement, shallow retention, and elevated dropout risk.
[277] This exposes a critical research gap: education systems need to move beyond static adaptivity and toward continuously personalized learning trajectories. Although adaptive testing and rule-based recommendation are well established, they remain episodic and limited in scope. What is still underexplored is the use of a continuous, self-optimizing Reinforcement Learning framework that can dynamically sequence an entire curriculum in real time, grounded in a rich and evolving model of each learner’s knowledge state. Addressing this gap is essential for building intelligent platforms that do not merely assess performance, but actively shape the most effective path to long-term mastery for every student.

[281] 3.1 Analysis/Framework
[282] The proposed system is built upon three foundational pillars designed to deliver a truly adaptive and personalized educational experience. The first pillar is the EdNet Dataset, a massive collection of over 130 million real-world student-item interactions [1]. This rich dataset provides the essential historical data needed to train a robust AI model that understands the intricate patterns of how students learn, progress, and struggle. By leveraging such a comprehensive dataset, the system can capture subtle trends and variations in student behavior across diverse backgrounds, subjects, and learning contexts, enabling more accurate modeling of individual learning trajectories. The second pillar is the AI Engine, which is powered by a Reinforcement Learning (RL) model. This engine acts as the brain of the system, continuously optimizing its teaching strategy. It takes the student's current knowledge state as input, decides on the optimal next action (e.g., advance, revise, or quiz), and learns from the outcome of that action. Through this ongoing process of feedback and adaptation, the AI engine becomes increasingly effective at personalizing instruction, identifying knowledge gaps, and maximizing each learner’s potential. The third and final pillar is the Interactive Platform, a seamless, user-friendly web portal that serves as the student's gateway to their personalized learning journey. This platform facilitates the interaction between the student and the AI engine, creating a fluid and intuitive educational experience. It not only delivers tailored content and assessments but also provides real-time feedback, progress tracking, and motivational support, ensuring that learners remain engaged and empowered throughout their educational journey.
[283] 3.2 Design Details
[284] The design of the system ensures a continuous and responsive loop between the student, the platform, and the AI engine. As students interact with the platform by completing lessons, answering questions, or seeking clarification, their actions and responses are instantly captured and analyzed. This real-time data is fed back into the AI engine, which dynamically updates its understanding of each learner’s knowledge state and adapts its instructional strategy accordingly. The platform then delivers new, tailored content or feedback based on these insights, creating an ongoing cycle of assessment, adaptation, and personalized support. This seamless feedback loop not only maximizes learning efficiency but also keeps students engaged and motivated by ensuring that every interaction is relevant to their unique needs and progress.
[285] GUI Design: The user interface will be a clean, modern, and intuitive web portal developed using React. Its design will prioritize simplicity and clarity, minimizing distractions to help students concentrate fully on their learning tasks. Interactive elements and navigation will be streamlined, ensuring that users can easily access lessons, quizzes, and progress information without unnecessary clutter. 
[286] After completing a question or module, the interface will prominently display the AI-recommended next step, guiding students seamlessly through their personalized learning journey. This approach makes the adaptive experience feel natural and engaging, fostering a sense of progression and motivation as students interact with the platform..

[295] 3.3 Methodology
[296] The core methodology of our system is based on a Reinforcement Learning framework. 
[297] State Representation: The "state" is a snapshot of the student's current knowledge and progress. It is represented as a feature vector that includes metrics such as the student's performance on recent questions, the time taken to answer, their historical mastery of different concepts (tags), and their current streak of correct or incorrect answers. This vector is constructed by the backend after each interaction.

This vector is constructed by the backend and sent to the RL agent to decide the next action.
[300] Action Selection: The “actions” represent the possible next steps the system can recommend to the student at any given moment. This set of actions includes advancing to a new concept when the student is ready, presenting a targeted question to help revise a topic that appears weakly understood, or assigning a focused quiz to reinforce and solidify knowledge across a bundle of related questions. The policy network within the Reinforcement Learning model analyzes the current state of the student-capturing their progress, strengths, and areas for improvement-and determines which action is most likely to produce a positive learning outcome. By continuously selecting and adapting these actions, the system ensures that each student receives guidance tailored to their evolving needs and learning trajectory.
[301] Example Scenario: Consider a student who has just incorrectly answered a question on 'linear-equations'. The system constructs the following state vector st​: {Last Answer Correctness: 0, Time Taken: 1.2 (20% slower than average), Correct Streak: 0, Mastery 'algebra': 0.85, Mastery 'linear-equations': 0.45, Recent Accuracy: 0.60}.
[302] Given this state, the agent's policy network analyzes the situation. It recognizes that while the student's general 'algebra' knowledge is strong (85% mastery), their understanding of 'linear-equations' is weak (45% mastery). The recent incorrect answer and slow response time confirm this. Therefore, taking the Advance action would be detrimental, as it would build new knowledge on a shaky foundation. The agent concludes that the most effective action, at​, is to Revise, selecting another 'linear-equations' problem to directly address and remediate this specific knowledge gap.
[304] Reward Function: The "reward" is a feedback signal that tells the AI engine how effective its last action was. The reward function is designed to incentivize actions that lead to improved student performance. For example, the agent receives a positive reward if the student successfully answers a question on a topic they previously struggled with after a revision action was recommended. This continuous cycle of state-action-reward allows the RL agent to refine its teaching strategy over time, becoming progressively better at personalizing the learning path.

[329] The foundation of our AI model is the EdNet dataset, a large-scale, hierarchical dataset containing 131,663,742 interactions from 784,309 students. The data is structured into two primary components:
[330] Student Logs (Knowledge Transfer 1 - Knowledge Transfer 4): These files contain the per-user interaction logs, which record detailed, timestamped data on each question a student attempts. Each entry includes information about the specific question solved, the time of the interaction, and mappings to relevant learning concepts and tags. This structure enables the system to track individual learning progress, identify patterns in student performance, and associate each response with particular knowledge areas, providing a rich foundation for personalized analysis and adaptive instruction.
[331] Question Metadata: This component provides detailed information about each question, including:
[332] question_id: A unique identifier for each question.
[333] bundle_id: Groups related questions into a single topic or lesson.
[334] part: A broader subject or chapter designation.
[335] tags: Specific, granular concepts tested by the question.
[336] To illustrate how this structure works in practice, consider a set of questions designed to teach the topic of "Java Variables".
[337] Part: Java Basics This represents the highest-level grouping, similar to a chapter in a textbook. It encompasses foundational concepts of the Java programming language, including variables, data types, operators, and control flow.
[338] Bundle ID: B_JAVA_VARS_01 This unique identifier represents a specific concept or lesson within the "Java Basics" part. In this case, it groups all questions related to the core concept of "Java Variables". This allows the system to identify that different questions are all testing the same fundamental skill.
[339] Questions and Tags: Within a single bundle, there are multiple individual questions, each with its own unique question_id and a set of descriptive tags. This granular level of detail is what allows the AI to discern specific knowledge gaps.

[384] 5.1 Data Preprocessing and Feature Engineering
[385] The EdNet dataset, consisting of over 130 million student-item interactions, was preprocessed to extract meaningful features for both the Student Simulator (LSTM) and the Reinforcement Learning agent (PPO). The preprocessing pipeline extracted five core features per interaction: correctness (binary), question difficulty (continuous, 0–1), response time (normalized), time lag (between interactions), and concept index (categorical). These features were organized into fixed-length sequences of 20 timesteps and saved as NumPy arrays.
[386] A difficulty map was computed from historical student performance data and stored, containing difficulty scores for all questions. Analysis of the dataset revealed a significant imbalance across the seven parts, with Part 5 alone containing 43.3% of all questions  more than the combined total of the next four largest parts. This distribution was accounted for during training to avoid bias toward over-represented topics.

[436] Early Phase (Steps 1–10): The agent assigned medium-difficulty questions (bin 5, ~0.5 difficulty). The student answered 8 out of 10 correctly, with the LSTM confidence rising from 0.62 to 0.68. When the student answered incorrectly at step 6 (mastery dropped from 1.0 to 0.9), the agent immediately escalated to a harder question (bin 9) to test limits.
[437] Mid Phase (Steps 11–25): The agent alternated between difficulty bins 5 and 9 depending on correctness streaks. The LSTM-predicted probability remained in the 0.55–0.68 range, keeping the student in the flow zone.
[438] Late Phase (Steps 26–50): As the student began failing more frequently (mastery dropping to 0.2–0.4), the agent consistently selected bin 9 (hardest difficulty). This resulted in extended failure streaks, suggesting a potential area for reward tuning: the current penalty structure reduced difficulty only after multiple failures at steps 30+.

[447] 5.6 Discussion
[448] 5.6.1 Strengths of the System
[449] 1. Superiority Over Baselines: The AI Teacher's 106% reward improvement over random selection and 89% over fixed-difficulty strategies demonstrates that the RL-based approach learns a meaningfully better teaching policy than naive alternatives.
[450] 2. Adaptive Responsiveness: The difficulty path visualizations confirm that the agent dynamically adjusts question difficulty based on student performance. The trained policy encodes the pedagogical principle of scaffolding-reducing difficulty after failures and increasing it after successes.
[451] 3. LSTM-RL Synergy: The two-stage architecture (LSTM simulator → PPO agent) proves effective. The LSTM provides a reasonably accurate student model (69.8% accuracy), and the PPO agent learns to leverage these predictions to select optimal difficulty levels.
[452] 4. Consistency: The AI Teacher's low variance in evaluation (Std Dev ~1.5) compared to baselines indicates that the learned policy is robust across different simulated student profiles, providing a consistently high-quality educational experience.
[453] 5.6.2 Limitations and Future Work
[454] 1. Difficulty Bias: The evaluation shows a tendency for the agent to favor high-difficulty questions (~0.9), which may not correspond to an optimal pedagogical strategy for all students. This could be mitigated by strengthening the flow zone bonus or introducing explicit curriculum constraints.
[455] 2. Simulator Accuracy Ceiling: The LSTM achieves ~69.8% accuracy, which means approximately 30% of simulated student responses are mismodeled. Future work could explore transformer-based architectures (e.g., SAINT, AKT) for improved knowledge tracing.
[456] 3. Limited Action Space: The current 10-bin difficulty discretization is relatively coarse. A continuous action space (using PPO with continuous actions) could allow for finer-grained difficulty tuning.
[457] 4. Single-Concept Sequencing: The current system selects difficulty but does not explicitly optimize the sequence of concepts. Future iterations could extend the action space to include concept selection alongside difficulty.

[468] Conclusion
[469] This project addresses a fundamental flaw in the rapidly growing e-learning industry: the prevalent "one-size-fits-all" approach to education. By treating all learners as a monolith, existing platforms fail to unlock the full potential of individual students, leading to disengagement and knowledge gaps. Our proposed solution is an intelligent platform that leverages a sophisticated Reinforcement Learning engine to provide a truly personalized and adaptive learning experience. By analyzing each student's unique interaction patterns, the system dynamically tailors the curriculum, guiding them on the most efficient path to mastery. This project's successful implementation will demonstrate the transformative potential of AI in education, creating a more effective, engaging, and equitable learning environment. The ultimate vision is to provide every learner with a personal AI tutor that understands their needs and helps them achieve their academic goals.
